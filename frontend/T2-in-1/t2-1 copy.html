THE
POPULATION
MONSTER

The birth of the Industrial Revolution in the mid 1700’s altered medicine and living standards, resulting in a population explosion that would steamroll into the 20th century. Between 1900 and 2000 the increase in world population was three times greater than during the entire previous history of humanity. This unprecedented growth threatened the lives of millions of people because of imminent food shortages, especially in developing countries. The monumental task of feeding the world population called for bold new humanitarian initiatives and cooperation between rich and poor nations. In the words of Norman Borlaug, this action was "a temporary success in man's war against hunger and deprivation," a breathing space in which to deal with the "Population Monster" and the subsequent environmental and social ills that too often lead to conflict between men and between nations.



Humanitarian foundations, governments, scientists and farmers came together in the first half of the twentieth century to find ways to conquer hunger. The International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center (CIMMYT) and its predecessor program the Office of Special Studies (OSS) were pioneers in efforts that would ultimately enable developing nations to achieve self-sufficiency in food production and gain entrance to world markets. These efforts provided a model that has since been widely adopted by both governments and non-government organizations, making a tangible difference in the lives of millions of people around the world.

MANUEL ÁVILA CAMACHO

Soldier and moderate statesman whose presidency in the period of 1940-1946 saw a consolidation of the social reforms of the Mexican Revolution and the beginning of an unprecedented period of friendship with the United States.

MEXICAN SECRETARIAT OF AGRICULTURE AND DEVELOPMENT

The Mexican Secretariat of Agriculture and Development, (now the Secretariat of Agriculture, Livestock, Rural Development, Fisheries and Food, or SAGARPA) was created in 1917 as part of the reforms resulting from the Mexican Revolution. It is responsible for administering federal resources to rural development, promotes research for rural purposes, regulates fishing activities and controls all medicines and food for domestic and farm animals.

A TOUR
THROUGH
MEXICAN
FARMLANDS
1940

When General Manuel Ávila Camacho became the President of Mexico on December 1, 1940, he took leadership of a country, that despite progressive reforms from the Mexican Revolution (1910-1920), still faced a bleak agricultural future: productivity faltered, infrastructure was lacking, technology and training opportunities were null or insufficient, and Mexico was unable to produce the food it needed.


Camacho and his cabinet member Marte R. Gómez, a distinguished Mexican agronomist and Secretary of Agriculture and Development, reached out to Henry Wallace, then Vice-President of the United States. Wallace had attended Camacho’s inauguration and had an extensive background in agriculture himself, having served previously as the U.S. Secretary of Agriculture, as well as being the founder and chairman of Pioneer Hi-Bred Seed and owner/editor of a well-known farming magazine Wallaces Farmer.

THE ROCKEFELLER FOUNDATION

From their very first grant —to the American Red Cross— through to the present-day initiatives, The Rockefeller Foundation has a legacy of trailblazing new fields, convening unlikely partners, and sparking new innovations that lead to transformative change. The Rockefeller Foundation took part in the modern field of public health, developing vaccines for diseases such as yellow fever and malaria, and partnered with the Mexican government and CIMMYT to spark the Green Revolution.

    GERMAPLASM

    Germplasm is living tissue from which new plants can be grown. It can be a seed or another plant part--a leaf, a piece of stem, pollen or even just a few cells that can be turned into a whole plant. Germplasm contains the information for a species’ genetic makeup, a valuable natural resource of plant diversity.

    1943

    They would collaborate with the Mexican Secretariat of Agriculture and Development to jointly work to:

  • Develop improved maize, wheat, and bean varieties.
  • Develop improved cropping and production practices (irrigation, pest and disease control, fertilizer use, chemical and mechanical weed control, planting dates, etc.).
  • Develop improved plant health practices.
  • Aiding in the development of animal husbandry practices.
  • Training Mexican agricultural scientists.
    • In 1943 this partnership was officially launched with the establishment of the Oficina de Estudios Especiales (OEE), or Office of Special Studies (OSS), whose goal was to ensure food security in Mexico and abroad through selective plant breeding and crop improvement programs. This project was the cornerstone of the Rockefeller Foundation’s Mexican Agriculture Program (MAP), and was the start of a nearly twenty year collaboration between the two countries. Under the leadership of the first director, Jacob George Harrar, prominent American agricultural researchers were brought to Mexico. They immediately began improvement programs for maize, wheat, and beans and for other important crops like barley, sorghum, potatoes, vegetables, and forage crops. Their early successes transformed agriculture in Mexico and paved the way for what was to become the Green Revolution. A maize germplasm collection consisting of Mexican and other Latin American maize landraces was also established in 1943. Today the CIMMYT germplasm bank contains 28,000 maize seed samples and 140,000 wheat seed samples, making CIMMYT the holders of the world’s largest collection of these two crops worldwide. This gene bank preserves the genetic diversity of maize and wheat and is an important resource for crop improvement and research.
    1944-1950
    THE
    EXPERIMENT
    STATION SYSTEM
    THE ROCKEFELLER FOUNDATION

    The Rockefeller Report drew particular attention to the lack of applied, practical field work at Mexican agricultural schools and the failures of research to take into consideration geographic and climatic diversity which made ideal plant varieties in one region unfit for another. One of the first initiatives of the OSS was to establish a network of experiment stations in distinct regions throughout Mexico. The country’s geographic diversity made it an ideal testbed for researching varieties of plants that could be grown in distinct regions of the globe. These extension stations provided Mexican agronomists with hands-on field experience that had been lacking up to that point in their mostly theoretical education. They also gave local farmers the opportunity to participate in research while learning about the latest cultivation methods that would best support their crops.

    Many of the original experimental stations remain some of the most important nationwide. These stations, each bearing unique climate, soil, and geographic characteristics, enabled research into new growing methods.

    • Campo El Horno, near Chapingo, Edo. Mexico established in 1944.
    • La Cal Grande, near La Piedad, Michoacán, established in 1952.
    • Campo del Valle del Yaqui, near Ciudad Obregón, Sonora established in 1955. As a further honor the experiment station was renamed Campo Experimental Norman E. Borlaug (CENEB) in 2010.
    • Cotaxtla, near Veracruz, established in 1955.
    • El Cayal, near Campeche, established in 1957.
    • La Campana, near Chihuahua, established in 1958.

    The OSS also operated the Santa Elena Experiment Station, established in 1952 in cooperation with the State of Mexico and collaborated at other stations in León, Guanajuato; Xalostoc, Morelos; and El Mexe, Hidalgo.

    TRAINING
    AND
    EXTENSION

    A generation of farmers and Mexican and Latin American agronomists were educated at the various experiment stations as part of a systematic effort to spread agricultural knowledge beyond the laboratory. Farmers were given the opportunity to actively participate in research, thus acquiring the management skills, access to tools, and familiarity with crop husbandry techniques that they lacked after the agrarian reforms of the Mexican Revolution.

    These sites also formed part of the ambitious effort to replace Mexico’s outdated educational system with a strategic pipeline that trained agronomists in accordance with the country’s specific agricultural needs. It would ensure that promising students had access to the in-depth, cutting-edge training they needed to fill the ranks of universities and governmental agencies capable of sustaining Mexico’s agricultural future.

    1950-1961
    THE ROCKEFELLER FOUNDATION

    Selected graduates from Mexican agriculture schools were invited to work as field assistants on the 150 acre farm donated to Chapingo University by the Mexican government. Afterwards, some were sent to the United States to pursue master’s degrees in need-based fields at prestigious universities such as Cornell and Harvard. After applying their studies in Mexico for a time, they were then given the possibility to pursue Ph.D’s at the best universities in their respective fields.

    By 1961, 750 Mexican technicians had received in-service training, 400 had obtained master’s degrees, and 75 had earned Ph.D.’s The pilot program also trained about 100 scientists from other parts of Latin America, prompting the first Latin American agricultural science meetings which took place in Mexico in 1949 and 1950. These meetings ultimately led to the foundation of the Latin American Agricultural Science Association (ALCA) to which hundreds of Latin American scientists now belong.

    DEVELOPING
    IMPROVED
    CROPS
    The scientists of the Oficina de Estudios Especiales (OEE) recognized the urgency of developing new and improved varieties to curb food shortages associated with population growth. They initiated research, using varieties from throughout the country and worldwide for crop improvement.


    This required meticulous collecting, cataloguing, cultivation and crossing of plants. Research was also subject to time, logistics and funding constraints with feedback from farmers required to validate their efforts. Eventually these efforts began to bear fruits. By applying technologies like modern irrigation methods, pesticides and fertilizers, higher yielding varieties were successfully bred and grown by farmers.

    ROCAMEX
    The first successful outcomes of extensive cross-breeding research were in maize, a project overseen by Edwin J. Wellhausen, head of the OSS’s maize research division. Wellhausen gathered over 800 Mexican varieties of maize and tested across a variety of conditions from varying temperature and precipitation to differing soil composition and altitude. He quickly achieved improvements to maize grain production simply by distributing the best Mexican maize varieties to farmers in regions that didn’t otherwise have access to them. Afterwards, he planted the best varieties next to each other, allowing natural cross-pollination to occur. The result was two open pollinated varieties. This second crossed line is known as a double-top cross synthetic, which had better yield than other varieties.


    By 1947 there were already ten new maize varieties available, and thanks to the Mexican government’s newly created Maize Commission, large-scale distribution of these high performing seeds was made possible. The first widely distributed seed was named “Rocamex” in honor of the joint endeavors of the Rockefeller Foundation and the Mexican government. In 1948, only five years after CIMMYT’s pilot program OSS was established, Mexico accomplished its first objective by achieving maize self-sufficiency.

    STEM RUST

    Rusts proliferate on a variety of grasses including on wheat and often will dramatically reduce grain yields. It can spread rapidly and over large distances by wind. It results in shriveled grains or causes the collapse of the stem that supports them, creating a devastating loss- seventy percent of more of entire crops can perish within a few weeks from harvest.

    FIGHTING
    RUST

    For thousands of years since the time wheat was first cultivated, a notorious, mold-like fungus called stem-rust , has posed the greatest worldwide threat to cereal crops. The ancient Romans held ritual sacrifices with the hope of halting its incursion and through the centuries scientists have struggled to prevent the devastating epidemics caused by this highly contagious plant disease.

    However, Borlaug made a breakthrough when he imported seeds containing genes from Norin 10, a new variety made by Japanese scientist Gonjoro Inazuka in 1935. This cross between two American wheat varieties and a semi-dwarf (+) Japanese landrace contained two critical genes, Rht1 and Rht2, which made it both shorter in stature, high-yielding, and high-tillering. Inazuka’s cross improved these dwarf varieties by further reducing the average height from 150 to 60-110 cm. This cross quickly attracted international attention and was brought to the United States by Samuel Salmon, a renowned wheat breeder in the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA). In the late 1940’s researcher Orville Vogel used it to produce high-yielding, semi-dwarf winter wheat varieties.

    THE DOUBLE WHEAT SEASON AND SHUTTLE BREEDING

    Borlaug was under pressure by project administrators to rapidly develop improved wheat varieties for Mexican farmers. To achieve this, he devised sowing and harvesting wheat twice within a 12 month period as various locations, in what has become known as shuttle-breeding. He was able to grow wheat in the Mexican highlands during the summer season and then grow a second crop in northwestern Mexico during the winter, hence doubling the efficiency of breeding new wheat varieties. This ingenious method had never been tried before and was initially dismissed by Borlaug’s OSS director, George Harrar, yet it resulted in broadly adapted, high yielding, disease resistant varieties. Shuttle breeding, effectively unlocked the barriers that had once impeded wheat sustainability on a global scale.

    AUTONOMOUS UNIVERSITY OF CHAPINGO

    Originally the National School of Agriculture (ENA), the Autonomous University of Chapingo was founded on February 22, 1854 in the Convent of San Jacinto, in Mexico City bearing the legacy of pre-Revolution Mexico. During the Mexican Revolution, the university was moved to the ex-hacienda of Chapingo, near Texcoco, where it adopted a new slogan: “Teach the use of the earth, not the exploitation of man.” The MAP’s merit-based mentorship of students catalyzed a long-term investment in infrastructure and capacity as it played a significant role in reconstituting this institution from the perspective of both human and agricultural advancement.

    CHAPINGO
    POSTGRADUATE
    COLLEGE AND LIBRARY

    The Mexican Agriculture Program (MAP) recognized that a vital part of sustaining agricultural advances was building a network of resources from which the next generations of farmers and young researchers could draw. The 150 acres donated by the Mexican government to the Office of Special Studies in 1943 at Chapingo University served as a base for field experimentation while also offering applied learning opportunities for promising students

    Chapingo University Library

    In 1945, Dr. Dorothy Parker established for the MAP a modern research library at Chapingo. It resulted in an impressive collection of 40,000 volumes that attracted researchers from across Latin America and served as a resource for teachers, researchers, students, and officials working within the National Center for Teaching, Research, and Agricultural Extension. In 1966 this library was donated to the National Agricultural Research Institute (INIA) where it was then transferred to Chapingo University and integrated into the National Agricultural Library.

    Post-graduate program

    On February 22, 1959, the Post Graduate School of the National School of Agriculture (later established as an independent institution) was founded, with the goal of advancing higher education in agriculture in Mexico. At first it offered Master’s degrees, but now also offers Ph.D’s. In 1978, the school was formally reestablished as the Autonomous University of Chapingo, which is today considered one of the leading agronomic institutions of higher learning in Latin America.

    THE END...
    AND THE BEGINNING

    Over the course of twenty years, the Office of Special Studies accomplished groundbreaking achievements in agricultural research and literally built an entire generation of Latin American agricultural experts from the ground up. Networks such as the Latin American Agricultural Sciences Association (ACLA) gave continued momentum to the field. Yet dire hunger, famine, and malnutrition coupled by climate variability and inconsistency and deepening economic disparities underscored the need for the OSS to broaden its agricultural advancements in wheat and maize. In 1960 seeds were made freely available to researchers worldwide through a network of international seed nurseries, and in 1961, the Office of Special studies officially came to a close as the International Center for the Improvement of Wheat and Maize (CIMMYT) was born.


    Many of the same researchers who worked with OSS continued their research under CIMMYT. Dr. Edwin J. Wellhausen, notable maize researcher and the former local director of the OSS, became the first Director General of CIMMYT. Among the others was Norman Borlaug, who as director of CIMMYT’s Wheat Program, drove the Green Revolution with the recognition of the 1970 Nobel Peace Prize. Many other staff were integrated into the newly established National Agricultural Research Institute (INIA) in Mexico, and in 1963 CIMMYT became an international agricultural research for development institution poised to guide agriculture into the twenty-first century.
